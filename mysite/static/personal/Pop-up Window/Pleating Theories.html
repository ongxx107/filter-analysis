Summary of Theories for Pleating Design

 

The web-based software for filter pleating design is based on the numerical model developed by Chen and Pui et al. (1993, 1994, 1995) for pleated filter panels operated at low flowrates; and the semi-empirical model by Chen et al. (1996) for their operation at high flowrates. A summary of those theories and models is provided below.

 

 

I.  Design of filter panel with rectangular pleat (low flowrate)

Chen et al. (1993, 1995) used the Galerkin finite element method to simulate the flow through pleated filter panels based on Darcy-Lapwood-Brinkman equation for flow inside the filter media and Navier-Stokes equations for flow outside the media. Figure 1 shows the computational domain and geometric symbols in this study. Figure 2 shows the general correlation curve.

 

Figure 1. Computational domain for rectangular pleat (Chen et al., 1995)

 

The set of equations was solved for rectangular pleated filter panels for six different filter media. A linear relationship between the inlet panel velocity and the pressure drop was found in this study for low inlet velocities (up to 250 fpm). III.  Pleating design at high flowrate (filter panel with triangular pleat)

Chen et al. (1996) investigated the effect of non-Darcy flow inside the filter medium and the turbulent flow within the pleat channel on pleating design at high flowrate. It was found that the pressure drop across the filter medium is no longer a linear function of the face velocity at high flowrate. Instead, it can be best fitted by a second-order polynomial,

as shown in Figure 4.

 

Also, at high flowrate the flow within the pleat channel is turbulent instead of laminar. Because turbulence results in an increased wall-shear stress and greater loss of fluid kinetic energy due to viscous dissipation, the pressure drop within the pleat channel at turbulent flow condition is larger than that at laminar flow condition.

 

     

Figure 4. Medium pressure drop vs. face velocity at high flowrate (Chen et al., 1996)

 

A semi-empirical model that takes into account the preceding factors was developed in this study for pleating design in high flowrate applications, such as cabin air filtration. A fitting procedure for the experimental data was used to determine the coefficients in the model. This model can be used to determine the best pleat number when either the pleat geometries or flow conditions change, and therefore reduce the future experiment runs for the same media. except here X = 2/(ΔPm) is not a dimensionless number anymore, and m1, m2 are experimentally determined coefficients. It was found that m1 is relatively independent of filter media, whereas m2 usually differs with filter media. The example value given by Chen et al. (1996) is m1 = 7.82 × 10-6Uin2, m2 = 8.96 × 10-10Uin3.28 for 3M 0104 media, 7.98 × 10-7Uin2.28 for 3M 0105 media. Note that the units of ΔP, W and Uin are mmH2O, cm and cm/s, respectively.
